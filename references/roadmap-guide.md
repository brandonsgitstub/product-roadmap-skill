# Roadmap Views — A Practical Guide

A roadmap communicates intent and sequence to an audience. The "right" format is the one that matches who's reading it and how much certainty the team actually has. This guide covers the three default views, when each fits, and the anti-patterns to avoid.

---

## View 1: Now / Next / Later

The default, and usually the best choice for internal alignment. Instead of dates, work is grouped into three relative horizons.

- **Now** — actively in progress or starting imminently. High confidence on both what and why. Small enough to be real (typically 2–5 initiatives, not 12).
- **Next** — the on-deck circle. Clearly intended, but not committed to a date. Often gated behind a Now item finishing or a piece of validation.
- **Later** — directional. Things we believe matter but haven't fully scoped, validated, or sequenced. Honest about uncertainty.

**Why it works:** it commits to direction without committing to false precision. Leadership gets a clear sense of focus; the team isn't held to dates invented before the work was understood.

**When to use:** fast-moving teams, internal stakeholders, anything early enough that quarter-level dates would be fiction.

**Watch for:** the "everything is Now" trap (see anti-patterns). Now is a statement of focus — if it's crowded, it's not focused.

---

## View 2: Theme-based

Organizes the roadmap around strategic themes — outcome-oriented buckets like "Activation", "Enterprise Readiness", "Performance & Reliability", or "Expansion Revenue" — rather than individual features.

Each theme holds the initiatives that serve it. The roadmap tells a story about *bets*, not a list of features.

**Why it works:** it keeps the conversation at the altitude leadership cares about. Execs rarely want the feature list; they want to know which strategic problems you're spending the quarter solving. Themes also make trade-offs legible: "we're going heavy on Activation this half, which means Enterprise waits."

**When to use:** roadmap reviews with leadership, board-adjacent communication, any time the narrative is more about strategic allocation than feature delivery. Often combined with Now/Next/Later *within* each theme.

**Watch for:** themes so broad they're meaningless ("Improvements", "Misc"). A good theme names an outcome you could succeed or fail at.

---

## View 3: Quarterly / Time-boxed

Initiatives mapped to specific quarters (or months). The most committal view.

**Why it works:** when there's a genuine external driver — a contractual deadline, a dependency chain with real lead times, a board commitment, a marquee customer's renewal — dates are the point, and pretending otherwise helps no one.

**When to use:** hard deadlines exist, cross-team dependencies need date coordination, or an external party is owed a timeline. Also useful late in planning when scope is well understood.

**Watch for:** date theater (see anti-patterns). Dates assigned to work nobody has scoped are a liability — they read as commitments and break as disappointments. Only time-box what's understood well enough to defend.

---

## Choosing between them

| If the audience is... | And certainty is... | Use |
|-----------------------|---------------------|-----|
| Internal team / leadership | Low to medium | Now / Next / Later |
| Leadership / board | Any | Theme-based (often + N/N/L inside) |
| Anyone owed a date | High on the near term | Quarterly / time-boxed |

These aren't mutually exclusive. A common strong combination: themes as the top-level structure, Now/Next/Later within each theme, and explicit dates only on the one or two items that genuinely have deadlines.

---

## Anti-patterns to avoid

**Everything is Now.** When a roadmap's Now column has ten items, it's communicating "we have no priorities," which is worse than communicating a hard choice. Now should hurt a little — it means saying not-yet to good ideas. If the PM resists trimming, surface the capacity math: how many of these can the team actually carry at once?

**Date theater.** Assigning precise dates to unscoped work to look organized. The dates are fiction, they get treated as promises, and the roadmap loses credibility the first time one slips. Only commit dates to work that's understood. For everything else, relative horizons are more honest and more durable.

**Themeless feature dump.** A flat list of features with no through-line. The reader can't tell what the strategy is, only what the tickets are. Even a simple Now/Next/Later beats this, because at least it communicates sequence. Group by theme or outcome wherever the work supports it.

**Hidden dependencies.** Sequencing items without surfacing what blocks what. The roadmap looks clean until the team discovers item B can't start until item A ships — and A is two horizons away. Always make dependencies explicit; they're the #1 cause of slipped roadmaps.

**The roadmap as a backlog.** A roadmap is a communication artifact with a point of view, not an exhaustive list of everything that might get done. If it can't be read and understood in a few minutes by someone who wasn't in planning, it's a backlog wearing a roadmap's clothes.
